Overwatch 2

4/10

Heavily Monetized

Release Date: Oct 4, 2022

Price: Free-to-play

ESRB Rating: Teen

Summary

Contains Battle Pass

Deceptive Pricing

No Loot Boxes

Overwatch 2 fails to provide all the information needed for consumers to make informed decisions on its in-game purchases. In addition, it incorporates deceptive designs to purposely obfuscate the value of its items and pushes users to spend more money using behavioral game design in its store front and battle pass system.

As a long-term player of Overwatch 1 and someone fully aware of Activision-Blizzard's heavy monetization of past games with World of Warcraft and Call of Duty, when they announced Overwatch 2 is going to be free-to-play, I and many others had concerns. While Overwatch 1 had the infamous loot boxes, it constantly rewarded the player with plenty of cosmetic items and out-of-game progression. However, with the on release price tag of $39.99 USD sliding down to zero for its sequel, it was difficult to become excited for a long-awaited sequel for fear of what it could turn into, especially after the slew of articles about issues in game development and leadership. Surprisingly, before the game was released Activision-Blizzard announced that no boxes would be found in Overwatch 2, a great move since loot boxes have been deemed psychologically similar to gambling, however Overwatch 2 monetization scheme incorporates even more deceptive designs made to push users to spend money, with some of them, like intermediate reinforcement, having the same addictive qualities as loot boxes. On release, players learned that there were even less rewards and out-of-game progression from playing the game as well. The change in business model from Overwatch 1 to Overwatch 2 led to an increase of monetization where cosmetics are almost exclusively locked behind paywall content. Its new battle pass system and storefront obfuscate the cost of the products using deceptive designs such as virtual currencies and incorporates physiological motivations like fear of missing out to push users into spending more money. These intentional choices in the game designs whittle away at consumers right and fail to value the players time and money.

Battle Pass Opinion

The business model change from pay to play to free to play has forced heavier monetization styles within the game and it is reflected clearly in the battle pass. It fits within the trend of " games as service" seen across the AAA industry where content is drip fed overtime to keep players engaged and find different forms of revenue. The battle pass is that reoccurring purchasing habit that Overwatch two hopes to encourage. This is made apparent through the promotion of the bottle pass with almost every section of the game. Gone are the days of account level which was a simple achievement of game experience that every player could proudly stand by to a temporary tier system that resets every 60 days. There are no more levels in Overwatch 2 and with that change the reward system for players as well. In Overwatch 1, players are awarded for playing the game with experience and levels every level the player completed they received a loot box which would contain four randomized cosmetics or currency to purchase a cosmetic of the player's choosing. Now with the battle pass system when players do their daily, weekly or even seasonal challenges and level up there may be no reward at all if they didn't pay into the premium battle pass. It is possible for players to level up their battle pass six times in a row without receiving a reward. Overwatch one consistently rewarded the player for playing the game and that system was replaced with semi-unpredictable rewards.

If you catalog the battle pass you'll notice that the free rewards are not evenly distributed throughout it. Instead sometimes players will get their free reward and just two levels all the way up to six. This is reminiscent of a deceptive design and psychological phenomenon known as intermittent reinforcement. Intermittent reinforcement refers to the delivery of a reward at irregular or unpredictable intervals. Intermittent reinforcement encourages the user to a specific desired task or behavior and a well-documented example of this is gambling. The battle pass also employs other psychological phenomena to encourage specific player behaviors and one of the largest known ones for battle passes is the fear of missing out. Players only have 60 days to complete all the tiers before all the items that they could not unlock disappear. Cosmetics and this battle pass are also set to never come back meaning if you do not unlock it within the 60 days you will never unlock it in the future. Players are encouraged to spend more time in the game due to the fact they feel they will be missing out on The limited cosmetics. The fear of missing out also encourages users to spend money on the battle pass. As they advance through the tiers and unlock the free cosmetics it also shows the premier awards they could unlock if they spent money. It teases and tends the user by dangling items they could have if they spend money. Despite all these designs meant to encourage user spending and Overwatch 2 battle passes an issue on consumers rights.

There are a multitude of concerns when it comes to the battle pass as a product. The battle pass is a non-refundable purchase, once you buy it you cannot get your money back. The premium battle pass advertises the sixty-two cosmetic items that you only unlock once you complete that tier. It is possible for you to pay for the premium pass and not get every item in the battle pass if you do not complete all the tiers within the 60-day timeline. It is possible for you to pay for a product, not receive all the items in that product and not get a refund. Consumers also cannot make an educated choice on if they can complete the battle pass in a timely manner or not. The amount of time it will take to reach all eighty tiers thus getting all the items in the premium battle pass is not listed anywhere. Users are unable to judge the time commitment the product demands to fully utilize its money's worth. Overwatch's battle pass uses deceptive designs to encourage more time in the game and to spend money on the battle pass along with failing to give consumers all the information they need to make an informed choice on the product.

In-Game Store

Overwatch 2 offers an in-game store where players can purchase cosmetics directly using a virtual currency. Compared to Overwatch one it offers a limited selection with a few single option purchases and a couple bundles. It is a rotating storefront with the items for sale changing every 24 hours or every 7 days. To gain virtual currency users must spend real world money. The graph below shows a breakdown of the cost of the currency plus the price per point ratio.

The game encourages users to make larger transactions to get a better price per point ratio. It is a type of waste aversion where players feel like they're not getting the best deal unless they spend more money. The storefront incorporates many designs and psychological effects to obfuscate the true value of the items and make it difficult for users to evaluate their purchase. The virtual currency attempts to obfuscate the true price of the items and disconnect users from its real-world cost. Saying skin cost 1,900 Overwatch Points to buy does not have the same intrinsic meaning as handing over $19 in cash. The store prices are also set up to always leave the user with leftover Overwatch Points. This is done to trigger the psychological tendency for waste aversion. By having those points there, the player will feel that they are being wasted despite them not being able to buy anything with it therefore are encouraged to spend more money so those leftover points aren't wasted. For example legendary skins are priced at 1900 Overwatch Points beating users to purchase the $20 bundle which provides 2,200 points leaving 300 left over from the purchase. In fact almost every purchase on the store will leave you with leftover currency. Below you will find a graph with the cost of every item in Overwatch 2.

Note that there are only three items that you can buy without any leftover currency out of the ten possible categories. These designs make it difficult for the players to effectively evaluate the product's value to them and our practices that would not be accepted outside of the digital format. It would be like a pizza parlor selling two slices of pizza for $4 but you can only hand them $5 bills. The end result is you paying $5 for those two slices of pizza despite it being only advertised as $4 for two and the same effect happens in Overwatch 2 storefront.

When Overwatch switched to its free-to-play model and included battle pass it also increased the pricing for all of its cosmetic items. Below is the graph comparing Overwatch 1 prices to Overwatch 2 prices for different categories of items.

When compared to Overwatch 1, every item either doubled or tripled in price without any distinctive justification. This pricing caused a lot of outrage among his player base and made users question the true value of what they are selling. A user on Reddit named RevJoyStick “calculated how much it would cost to buy every item” they own from Overwatch 1 into Overwatch 2 store prices excluding sprays. According to their visual graph the cosmetics in their account are supposedly valued at over $10,000 at Overwatch’s pricing.

The game does not reveal the amount of money it is possible to spend on my cosmetics when players start playing the game. Let us say a player wanted one legendary skin for all of their characters. There are thirty-six characters in Overwatch 2 and legendary skins cost $19. That would come to a total price point of 684 dollars just to have one skin for every character. It is possible to earn Overwatch Points through in-game mechanics but it is at a glacially slow-pace. The player can earn up to 60 Overwatch points per week by completing all the weekly challenges. It would take a player 9 weeks of continually completing all the weekly challenges to afford one legendary skin in Overwatch 2 from the storefront.

Outside of pricing, Overwatch to storefront encourages user spending similarly like the battle pass by invoking the fear of missing out. Its store selection is extremely limited, only offering up to a bundle or items at a time half of which are on a rotating timer. Every 24 hours four items change to something completely different. It is the same monetization method that Fortnite uses for its door front and has found massive success with it. Because users never know when those items will appear in the store again, if they see something they like they are pushed to purchase it for the fear of never seeing it again. It creates a level of artificial scarcity for the cosmetic items despite its easily reproducible digital format.

Conclusion

Overwatch 2 fails to provide all the information needed for consumers to make informed decisions on its in-game purchases. In addition, it incorporates deceptive designs to purposely obfuscate the value of its items and pushes users to spend more money using behavioral game design in its store front and battle pass system. Its battle pass poses an issue for consumers right to a product they purchase by having a built in expiration date meaning that players can purchase the $10 USD premium battle pass without receiving all its products with no available refund. In addition to having the cosmetic store and the battle pass, Activision-Blizzard nearly doubled the price of all the cosmetic items in their store, over inflating their value and allowing users to spend hundreds, if not thousands of dollars on their store with no limits or warnings in place. Despite the fun team-based gameplay and the wonderful people you can meet through its custom game modes, I would recommend refraining from spending any money on Overwatch 2 until these issues are addressed.
